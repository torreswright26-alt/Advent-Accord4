# Render Style Bible

## Primary Benchmark
The current primary benchmark is approval `002`, the intimate sunset street scene.

Its approved qualities:
- painterly selective detail,
- moderate complexity,
- lighting that is dramatic but readable,
- colors that pop without becoming oversaturated,
- varied skyline and immediate-location contrast,
- experimental camera placement,
- strong foreground enclosure opening toward a larger world,
- lived-in architecture,
- reduced particulate density.

## Core Priority: Painterly Before Realistic
The target is stylized painterly concept art, not photorealism and not noisy AI painting.

When painterly expression and photorealistic fidelity conflict, preserve painterly expression.

Prefer:
- visible but intentional brush masses,
- expressive simplification,
- strong shape language,
- selective lost-and-found edges,
- painted foliage, stone, water, clouds, and atmosphere,
- artistic interpretation over physically exhaustive material rendering.

Avoid:
- photographic material simulation,
- lens-first realism,
- uniformly resolved surfaces,
- procedural-looking texture,
- tiny marks used to imitate craftsmanship.

## Detail Density and Focal Hierarchy
The goal is not low detail. The goal is intentional allocation of detail.

Use a clear hierarchy:
- focal area: highest edge fidelity, material definition, ornament, contrast, and narrative information,
- secondary area: readable but simplified,
- background: broad value masses, softer edges, atmospheric perspective, simplified silhouettes,
- far distance: nearly abstract, carrying scale and mood rather than object-level information.

A useful quality check is to blur or reduce the image to roughly 20% size. One focal point should remain clear.

Complexity should come from design, silhouette, architecture, composition, and storytelling—not from texture density.

## High-Frequency Visual Noise
Avoid incidental high-frequency visual noise that does not contribute to the scene.

This includes:
- random glowing flecks,
- decorative floating particles,
- scattered micro-specular highlights,
- arbitrary debris,
- texture-like sparkles,
- uniformly distributed tiny contrast accents,
- repetitive micro-marks filling negative space.

Atmosphere should be communicated primarily through value grouping, lighting, depth, edge control, composition, and purposeful environmental effects.

Weather and environmental particles such as rain, snow, ash, pollen, embers, and mist must behave as believable physical phenomena and remain compositionally intentional.

Quiet surfaces and visual rest areas are essential.

## Surface Treatment
Architecture and props should read as large painted planes first.

Prefer:
- broad plaster, stone, timber, cloth, and metal masses,
- subtle variation over large surfaces,
- wear concentrated where physically and narratively justified,
- ornament reserved for focal or culturally significant areas,
- brush strokes that follow form.

Avoid:
- repeating polygon-like patches,
- tiled procedural texture,
- every wall cracked or chipped,
- every stone individually described,
- uniform edge breakup,
- repeated geometric marks masquerading as surface richness.

## Value Grouping
Prefer:
- large connected shadows,
- broad midtone fields,
- controlled highlights,
- clear light direction.

Avoid:
- rapid pixel-to-pixel value oscillation,
- noisy edge breakup across the entire image,
- equal contrast everywhere.

## Edge Hierarchy
- Hardest edges near focal subjects and key architectural boundaries.
- Softer edges in atmosphere, distant buildings, foliage, clouds, and secondary props.
- Lost edges are desirable where they support depth or mood.

## Clouds and Natural Forms
Natural forms must feel shaped by physical flow and painterly intent.

For clouds:
- preserve convincing volume,
- use large masses with internal variation,
- use directional brushwork shaped by wind,
- combine soft gradients with selective crisp light edges,
- vary cloud structure across weather conditions.

Avoid:
- repeated circular cloud puffs,
- cotton-ball construction,
- stamp-like rounded masses,
- blurry streaks without volume,
- fields of similarly sized cloud blobs.

Apply the same principle to smoke, fog, foliage, cliffs, dunes, water, and other natural forms.

## Color, Lighting, and Emotional Impact
Reducing noise must never automatically reduce color, awe, sorrow, beauty, or dramatic lighting.

Keep:
- rich color relationships,
- dramatic but readable light,
- strong warm/cool contrast,
- saturated local accents,
- expressive skies,
- emotional atmosphere,
- scale and wonder.

Dark fantasy is a property of the world, not the exposure. Danger, mystery, corruption, false safety, sacred decay, and awe may appear in bright daylight, vivid color, snow glare, clear weather, celebration, or tranquil settings.

Avoid defaulting to:
- monochromatic darkness,
- teal-orange repetition,
- gray overcast with warm lantern accents,
- golden-hour spectacle as the universal solution.

## Composition
Every approved training image should contribute a meaningfully different composition.

Vary:
- centered and asymmetrical arrangements,
- top-down and low-angle views,
- intimate eye-level scenes,
- doorway and window framing,
- seated or ground-level cameras,
- rooftop and balcony views,
- close architectural studies,
- compressed telephoto views,
- expansive establishing shots.

Do not repeatedly default to:
- elevated overlook,
- lone cloaked traveler,
- giant city or citadel,
- leading path,
- dramatic clearing sky.

## Single-Image Rule
Every training reference must contain one full-frame image, one coherent place, and one composition.

Reject:
- collages,
- grids,
- triptychs,
- contact sheets,
- split screens,
- inset images,
- moodboards,
- multiple unrelated scenes in one panel.

## Subject and Scene Diversity
Characters are optional. Environment-only, architecture-only, interior-only, and location studies are strongly encouraged.

Across the approved dataset, deliberately vary:
- location and environment type,
- weather, season, time of day, and mood,
- interior and exterior settings,
- camera height, distance, lens feeling, and orientation,
- architecture, materials, culture, technology level, and scale,
- crowd density and ordinary daily activity,
- character age, body type, occupation, silhouette, wardrobe, hair, eyes, and cultural styling,
- manifestation presence, intensity, color, behavior, and medium.

Variation must produce distinct visual stories rather than cosmetic changes to the same scene template.

Every approved image should contribute a new visual idea, not merely a new composition.

## Environmental Identity
A location should remain visually itself even when characters or manifestations are present.

The dataset must not collapse into castles, harbors, mountains, valleys, green landscapes, warm interiors, or medieval-European cityscapes. Interiors, ordinary homes, workshops, streets, wetlands, deserts, frozen regions, ruins, civic spaces, underground spaces, and culturally distinct built environments all matter.
